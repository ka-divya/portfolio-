Book Nook

A reading tracker where your books grow like a garden.

Timeline 2026 · ~3 weeks, end to end
Role Solo · concept, illustration, design & development
Type Self-initiated passion project
Stack Figma · React · Next.js · Tailwind · Supabase · Framer Motion · Vercel · Claude Code · Cursor
Status Live web app ↗
Overview

A reading tracker where your books grow like a garden.

I grew up loving books. As an adult, I've found it hard to hold onto that. Life gets busy, and reading quietly slips down the list. Lately I've been trying to find my way back to it, and when I looked for an app to help, everything felt the same: data-driven, list-shaped, a little soulless. Tracking my reading that way made it feel like one more thing to log, not something to look forward to. So I decided to design the app I actually wanted.

Book Nook treats reading as something you tend, slowly, over time. Your books grow like a garden, where each month is a pot and each book a flower. A book you're reading is a bud. A book you finish blooms. A book you abandon quietly wilts. It's a small, cozy world: a room with a window, a cat beside you, a toggle for day and night, designed to make tracking feel less like logging and more like coming home to a quiet space that's yours.

And I didn't stop at the design. I illustrated every element by hand in Figma, designed the experience, and built it into a live, working web app. Concept to craft to shipped product, solo, in about three weeks.

The Idea

Every other app logs your reading. This one lets it grow.

Reading trackers are built like spreadsheets. You finish a book, you add a row, a number ticks up. They're efficient, and they're joyless. Nothing about them reflects what reading actually feels like: slow, personal, something that accumulates quietly into a part of who you are.

Book Nook is built on one idea. What if your reading wasn't a list you maintained, but a garden you grew? A book in progress is a bud, full of potential. Finish it and it blooms. Set it aside for good and it wilts, an honest little record that not every book is for you. Your year becomes a row of pots, each month its own small bloom of what you read.

The whole design follows from one shift: from tracking books, to tending them.
The World

A room you'd actually want to spend time in.

Book Nook isn't a screen, it's a place. The home view is a cozy brick-walled nook: a sunlit window with a hand-drawn sky, a wooden sign that takes on your name so the space is genuinely yours, a watering can spilling flowers on the wall, and a ginger cat asleep in the corner, tail flicking. That cat became the app's mascot. I love cats, so I drew one into the heart of Book Nook and carried it through the login and signup screens too, in a different pose each time. Everything is illustrated by hand in Figma, down to the curtain folds and the grain on the shelves.

Step inside first. The live app, in motion. (Click the video to pause.)

Twelve pots line three wooden shelves, one for every month. I gave each pot its own face and personality on purpose. A garden of identical pots would feel mass-produced, but a shelf of little characters, one grinning, one sleepy, one mid-wink, feels hand-tended and alive, like a collection you've gathered rather than a grid you've filled. The flowers follow the seasons where they can, so spring pots bloom with tulips and pansies and later months bring sunflowers and gerberas, with a few chosen just for the joy of variety.

No two pots alike. The shelf in the live app (left), and the hand-drawn pot & flower system in Figma (right).

The room also responds to you. A toggle flips the whole nook between day and night, shifting the lighting and hue and changing the scene in the window, where the sun gives way to a moon. It's a small touch, but it's the difference between a static screen and a space that feels lived-in.

One toggle, two moods. The sun clocks out, the moon takes over.

How It Works

The garden isn't a skin. It's the system.

It would be easy to make a pretty theme sit on top of an ordinary tracker. I wanted the metaphor to go deeper than that, so the garden and the data are the same thing.

Here's the flow. The room is your home. Tap any month's pot and it opens to that month's books, a clean, readable list where the real work happens: adding a title, writing your notes, giving it a rating. The whimsy gets you in the door; the list is where you actually manage your reading. Knowing when to switch from delight to plain usability was one of the more useful design calls in the project.

From the garden, to the shelf, to the book. Tap a month to open its list; tap a book for its own quiet page.

And in motion: planting a new book, from the shelf to its page. (Click the video to pause.)

The two layers stay connected through status. Every book is in one of three states, and that state is its flower. A book you're reading sits as a bud. Mark it finished and the bud blooms on the shelf. Set it aside and it wilts. Change how you're reading, and the garden changes with it. Your shelf is never a decoration you maintain separately; it's a live picture of where your reading actually stands.

Status isn't a label. It's the state of the flower. (Click the video to pause.)

Building It

Designed it, then built it for real.

I didn't want Book Nook to stop at a prototype. I wanted something I could send someone a link to and have them actually use, so I took it all the way to a working product.

The stack is a real one. React and Next.js for the app, styled with Tailwind, with Supabase handling accounts and storing every reader's books, Framer Motion driving the animations like a bud blooming into a flower, and the whole thing deployed live on Vercel. I built it in Claude Code and Cursor, doing the hands-on coding and edits myself while directing every design and product decision, until the thing actually ran and shipped.

Doing this changed how I design. Once you know an idea has to become real, you start thinking in terms of what the data actually is, what a state really costs, and where a lovely concept meets a hard constraint. The bud-bloom-wilt animation looked simple in Figma, but making it feel right in motion, tied to real status changes from a real database, was its own kind of problem. Designing and building the same product taught me to respect both sides of that line.

The home view, shipped for real. Twelve months, twelve hand-drawn pots, one quiet room.

Reflection

A small project that taught me a lot.

Book Nook started as a way back to something I'd loved, and it turned into the project where the most came together at once. I illustrated it, designed it, and built it into something real, and each of those pushed on the others in ways I didn't expect. Drawing every pot made me care about the details; having to build it made me honest about what those details cost.

The best part was getting to use both halves of my training at once. My background is technical, but design is where I want to build my craft, and this project let the two meet. Being comfortable with the engineering meant I could design knowing what was real, what a state actually costs, and where a lovely idea meets a hard constraint.

If I keep going with it, the natural next step is a mobile version, since a cozy reading nook belongs in your pocket, and maybe a little more life in the room over time. But even as it is, it's the piece I'm happiest to just hand someone and say: open it, it's yours to wander around.
