FineChef

A cooking app that removes the guesswork, not the joy.

Timeline 8 weeks · concept → tested → refined
Role Sole Designer · Research, UX, UI, Prototyping
Team Solo project
Tools Figma · Miro · Canva
Status Concept · 2024
Overview

A cooking app that removes the guesswork, not the joy.

FineChef is a mobile cooking app for people who want to get a good meal on the table without the friction that usually comes with it. The primary user is someone busy: they don't have time to decode a vague recipe mid-cook, but it's built to grow with them so the occasional cook who's starting to explore feels just as at home.

Most cooking apps hand you a wall of instructions and walk away. The hard parts — how much, how long, did I already make this? — are left to you. FineChef is designed around those exact moments: clear quantities and timing when you're in the thick of it, and a memory of what you've cooked and loved so you're never starting from zero.

As sole designer, I ran the project end to end over 8 weeks — interviews and a competitive audit, personas, wireframes through hi-fi, and a clickable prototype I tested with real users and refined.

The Problem

Floury hands, vague recipe, no clue.

In interviews, the same frustration kept surfacing in two different disguises. People weren't bad at cooking. They were being made to do unnecessary work by the apps meant to help them.

The first was ambiguity in the moment. Recipes routinely skip the things you actually need mid-cook like exact quantities, real timings, what "until done" means. So people improvise, second-guess, and stall, dish in hand, phone covered in flour. The instruction is there; the clarity isn't.

The second was amnesia across time. Every app treats each cook as a blank slate. The dish you nailed last month, the one you want to make again, gone. There's no memory of what you've tried, liked, or want to return to, so cooking never compounds into a habit. You're always restarting.

Underneath both is one problem: cooking apps make you start from scratch every time — within a single recipe, and across your whole journey. FineChef set out to fix the starting-from-zero, in both directions.

"Imagine watching the whole video, only to realise you can't make it."
— Interview participant, on recipes that leave out what you need
Research

Three things people kept telling me.

5 one-on-one interviews · competitive audit of 3 leading cooking apps

1. The hard part isn't cooking. It's the uncertainty.
People weren't afraid of the work but rather they were tired of guessing. Vague quantities and timings turned simple dishes into a series of small judgment calls, and each one was a chance to get it wrong. Confidence, not skill, was the gap.

2. Effort wasted late hurts most.
The worst moment wasn't a hard recipe — it was realizing too late that you couldn't make it. A missing ingredient or an unclear step discovered halfway in didn't just cost time; it cost trust in the app.

3. Cooking should compound. It doesn't.
The people who loved cooking felt stuck in their own rotation — able to repeat what they knew, but with nothing helping them build on it or branch out. Every app forgot what they'd done the moment they closed it.

The audit confirmed it: across the apps I audited, every one made you choose between watching and reading — flip to the video, lose your place in the steps, flip back. None let you do both at once. The market was full of recipes and empty of support while you actually cook.

Personas

One core problem, two people who feel it differently.

The same frustration — being made to start from scratch — showed up in two kinds of cook. I designed primarily for one, while making sure the other still felt at home.

PRIMARY · Ravi (the busy cook)
Ravi is who FineChef is built for. He cooks after a full day, with limited time and low patience for ambiguity — he doesn't want to discover mid-recipe that something's unclear or unmakeable. His need for clarity and zero wasted effort drove the core decisions: the upfront preview, exact quantities and timing, and Cook Along.

SECONDARY · Seema (the cooking-lover)
Seema represents the cook FineChef should grow with. She cooks for pleasure and wants to expand beyond her usual rotation, but nothing helps her explore or remember what she's tried. Designing for Ravi's clarity served her too — and Cook Book is where her need to build on past cooking gets met.

The Solution

One app that stays with you mid-recipe and over time.

FineChef is built around the two moments other apps abandon: when you're standing in the kitchen unsure what to do next, and when you want to build on what you've already made. Three features carry that idea.

Feature 1 · Cook Along
The recipe page opens like a video you can actually cook to: the demo stays pinned at the top while the written steps scroll beneath it, so you never flip between watching and reading again. Optional audio reads each step aloud — hands-free, for when yours are covered in flour.

[ IMG · Cook Along screen — your biggest visual (video mockup on web) ]

Cook Along: the demo stays pinned while steps scroll beneath. Watch and read at once.

Feature 2 · Everything you need, up front
Before you commit, the recipe shows exact quantities, real timing, and what you'll need — so you know you can make it before you've invested twenty minutes finding out you can't.

[ IMG · upfront recipe view (quantities, timing, ingredients) ]

Everything you need to know before you start — quantities, timing, ingredients.

Feature 3 · Cook Book
When a dish is done, one tap saves it to your Cook Book with a quick rating. Over time it becomes a personal record of what you've made and loved — so cooking compounds instead of resetting.

Testing & Iteration

I watched five people move through it. Then I changed what wasn't working.

I ran moderated usability sessions — watching people work through the prototype rather than just asking their opinion. Some changes came straight from what I saw them do; others from thinking harder about the real context the app would live in.

Change 1 · A preview before you commit
What I saw: People tapped into recipes blind, then backed out once they realized it wasn't what they wanted. The exact frustration from my interviews was happening in front of me.
What I changed: A preview pop-up on tap — image, cook time, rating, description — so you can size up a recipe first. Testing pushed it further: people still wanted to know what they'd need, so I pulled ingredients into the preview too.

[ IMG · Before → After: recipe preview pop-up + ingredients ]

Now you know if a recipe is right — and if you can make it — before opening it.

Change 2 · Listen, don't just read
What I realized: A prototype gets tested at a table, but this app gets used in a kitchen. Which means hands busy, often messy, attention split. Tapping through steps mid-cook wouldn't hold up there.
What I changed: I added audio narration to Cook Along so steps can be heard, not just read. A practical fix for real kitchen use that doubled as an accessibility gain — better for people who process by listening, or with visual impairments.

[ IMG · Before → After: audio narration added to Cook Along ]

Designed for where it's actually used — and more accessible because of it.

Change 3 · Color with the energy of food
What I reconsidered: The pastel palette was low-contrast and felt wrong for the product — cool and muted where food should feel warm and energetic.
What I changed: A saturated teal-and-orange palette that cleared contrast standards and gave the brand real appetite.

[ IMG · Before → After: color scheme (pastel → saturated) ]

Accessibility and appetite, in one palette.

Accessibility shaped these calls rather than capping them off: audio for different ways of processing, contrast for readability, plain language throughout.

Reflection

What testing taught me, and what I'd do next.

The biggest shift came from testing. I'd built the flow around assumptions from my research, but watching real people use it showed me what was actually working and what wasn't — things I couldn't have caught from my own screen. The preview pop-up, my most important change, only existed because I saw people struggle without it.

If I took this further, the first thing I'd do is close the loop: I changed three things based on testing, but I haven't re-tested those changes yet. I'd want to put the updated flow back in front of users to confirm the fixes actually solved the problems, rather than assuming they did.

Beyond that, I'd build out Cook Book: the saving-and-rating feature has the most room to grow into real personalization, where the app starts to understand your taste and help you build on what you've cooked.
